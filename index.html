Elementary Stochastic Processes

ELEMENTARY STOCHASTIC CALCULUS — THE COIN-TOSSING EXPERIMENT

Toss a fair coin n times over a total time horizon T. Heads you win, tails you lose. In the basic game the stake is ±$1 per toss; in the refined game it is ±√(T/n), so that the variance of the final winnings stays equal to T no matter how many steps you take. As n → ∞, the path of Si approaches Brownian motion.

In 1827 the botanist Robert Brown watched pollen grains jiggle erratically in water — battered from every direction by invisible molecules. This is Brownian motion: the continuous-time limit of our coin-tossing walk. Below, a particle wanders in the plane; each coordinate is an independent Brownian motion, and its trace is the blue thread of pure randomness.

The same Brownian engine dX drives every model in this book — what changes is the drift and how the noise enters. Three canonical processes, one shared source of randomness: the arithmetic random walk (can go anywhere, even negative), the lognormal walk of stock prices (noise proportional to the level, always positive), and the mean-reverting Vasicek walk of interest rates (pulled back toward its long-run level θ like a spring).
